Pass Rates, Retention and "Dropouts"

Every year, almost without fail, when the matric results are released, our media and commentati alight with opinions on the "real (matric) pass rate". This "real rate" is calculated taking number of matric students that passed (or wrote), and dividing it by either the number of Grade 1, 2 or 10 learners from that cohort.

Now, I do believe that such measures are worthwhile, but calling it the "real pass rate" is incredibly misleading. Then referring to the "missing learners" (as this percentage is never 100%, so learners had to have disappeared, right?) as "dropouts", and "never completing" is not just misleading, it's factually false.

Though the leaders of the Commentati are referring to cohort drop out when they just say "drop out", this key word doesn't seem to filter through to the larger public, who seems to be under the impression that the "drop out" mentioned in the press releases means learners are leaving the system and never finishing their education. Letâ€™s put that to bed right now. According to the Department of Basic Education, supported by figures from Statistics South Africa, almost 100% of learners of compulsory school-going age are in school. This could never be the case if learners dropped out and left the system.

I am not going to delve into the quality of matric, or debate the merits of different measures. The point I wish to make here is that compounding a pass rate year after year can have a large effect over the 12 years of schooling.

Suppose 1 000 000 learners entered Grade 1 and that for the next 12 years, the pass rate for each year was the same. For a given pass rate, what could one reasonably expect for the cohort percent that makes it to matric in the minimum specified time?

I will point out here, that we will be looking at percentages, so the number of learners starting does not matter for our analysis.

It must be noted, that we use Grade 2 as a base (instead of Grade 1), because it is acknowledged that a lot of learners repeat Grade 1 - a lot of learners also repeat Grade 2, by the way.

As you can see, even with an annual pass rate of 95%, only 43.12% of learners would make it to matric from Grade 2 in the minimum amount of time.

Even with a rather high pass rate of 95%, only 57% of learners would complete their school in the minimum amount of time. But that number would increase rather significantly to 88% if you allow one extra year, and to 98% if you allow 2 extra years.

There is a lot of evidence pointing to the pass rate per year being below 95%, and it varies quite a lot from year to year. The point I wish to make here is that even under the assumptions of a good pass rate, a significant number of learners will not make it to matric in the minimum amount of time - the measure the Commentari holds so dear.

12 years is a long time, and compounding can have large effects.
